Packet Switching in the 1960s

The internet has undergone a lot of trials and errors and there are a lot of revolutionary inventions that contributed to how we know it today. This writing will focus on one major moment, the invention of packet switching and its benefits. The invention that brought about the development of the first web browser, the WorldWideWeb. Packet switching can be defined as the transfer of sliced out small pieces of data (packets) across a wide range of networks. These packets travel via a medium that allows multiple users to do transmissions simultaneously (Davies, 2001).

After the giant ENIAC machine had been invented, the first significant computer (Hartree, 1946) and smaller other ones later, there was notion in the technology fraternity regarding the means of sharing data from one computer to the next. Although there are a lot of names around packet switching as well as uncertainties regarding who came up with the idea first. Considering the time of events, Paul Baran was the first ever person to come up with the idea and execute it in the early 1960s. After graduating as an electrical engineer in 1959, Baran went to work for RAND Corporation and created a communication network.

A communication network that had divided communication streams that were message blocks or “packets” (National Inventors Hall Of Fame, 2007). This is what a package switching system is. Packet switching was not considered by the society then until the mid-late 60s. Packet switching started gaining traction when a team of developers at the National Physical Laboratory had arguably the same aspirations as Paul Baran, to transfer data over networks and make it convenient in terms of the amount of time the data will take to get to the other end. During that time, the phenomenon of transferring data was just being introduced with telephones by the Post Office, this was through circuit switching. With computers, it was being introduced just not for communication purposes or rather it had not gained traction (Paul Baran).

Donald Davies a computer scientist employed at the National Physical Laboratory, while working at the labs observed that large frame computers would slice up programs for the benefit of the operators rather than having a long message on the line that caused a delay. He figured why not apply the same system for communication purposes. It is not known whether he had any influence from Baran’s work or not but upon realizing that, himself and a team started working on a prototype for the system in 1965. Packet switching meant that the transferring of data could be faster. They built this system and first it was operating just for the NPL. It was later considered by the Post Office and connecting networks began there (National Physical Lab, 2011).

Packet switching does not use a phone-like system like other methods, where only two networks can connect to one another at a time (Davies, 2001). As mentioned above, multiple users can do transmissions simultaneously to the same network. Packet switching is a significant moment because of how much it become a stepping stone for the creation of not just the www but other web browsers. There would have been no need of the www without the interconnection from packet switching. A lot of people are on web for multiple reasons but sharing data over the internet especially to or from different networks at the same time is one of them. The www is the blueprint of all the web browsers we use today to access social media platforms that have taken the world by storm. Packet switching also contributed to why the web is considered one of the most impactful inventions. In the past, people would send letters to one another via the post office and hope the other person receives it. The chances of them not were great.

In conclusion, although packet switching is not the only way of transferring data over networks, it has though proved to have made a huge contribution to how we know the internet today, packet switching is efficient and has completely influenced how networks are being built today.
